These are good times for all the boys who've been crying wolf about things since 2008. Most of those things are starting to come true. The inflation boys had been crying wolf since 2008. It only took 14 short years, but they were finally proved right. They were heard whispering, “I told you so.” They couldn't shout because their throats had gone hoarse from crying wolf for 1.4 decades. 

The "a sovereign debt crisis is coming" boys have been crying wolf for longer, but a crisis never came. But it finally looks like their time is about to come. I'm writing this because for the last couple of months, I've been listening to the various macro commentators, and now I think the end of the world, including a sovereign debt crisis, is just 2 quarters away. 

[The IMF, World Bank, etc., have all raised concerns about debt distress in emerging markets in the last month. But there's nuance here. Not all emerging markets and developing markets are the same. If this was the 2000s, we could've probably cried wolf about a blanket emerging markets sovereign debt crisis, but things have changed quite a bit since then.][1]

Emerging markets saw a series of defaults from the 1970s to the early 2000s. The first wave of crises occurred between 1970-80 in Latin America. In the early 1970s, real interest rates were low and even negative. At the same time, thanks to the OPEC oil shocks, oil-exporting countries were enjoying record surpluses. Given that oil was denominated in dollars and mostly deposited with US banks, this money was looking for yield. During the 70s, Latin American economies were growing quite well. Given the cheap dollar loans, these countries borrowed heavily to invest in infrastructure, military and other public spending. Given the growth, they could sustain the large deficits. External debt in Latin America shot up from $32 billion in 1970 to $332 billion in 1983. Latin American debt accounted for [176%][3] of the capital of the 9 of the largest US banks. 

<img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1024" height="606" src="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Latin-american-debt-crisis-1024x606.png" alt="" class="wp-image-304" srcset="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Latin-american-debt-crisis-1024x606.png 1024w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Latin-american-debt-crisis-300x178.png 300w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Latin-american-debt-crisis-768x455.png 768w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Latin-american-debt-crisis.png 1245w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /> [Chang Wei Liang][4] 

The 70s and 80s were a stubbornly high inflationary period for the US. In 1979 Paul Volcker was appointed to tame inflation. Volcker promptly raised the interest rates to 20%, which put the US in recession and caused a global slowdown. Most of the debt of the Latin American countries was short-term with floating rates. Suddenly, the debt servicing costs for Latin American counties shot up, currencies saw sharp devaluations, and they had to [default][5]. 

<img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="768" height="576" src="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/US-interest-rates-debt-crises.webp" alt="" class="wp-image-256" srcset="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/US-interest-rates-debt-crises.webp 768w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/US-interest-rates-debt-crises-300x225.webp 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 768px) 100vw, 768px" /> [Brookings][6] 

The next major episode of the sovereign debt crisis was the Asian Financial crisis. South Korea, Indonesia and Thailand were at the heart of the crisis, which later hit the Philippines, Malaysia, and Hong Kong. In the 90s, low interest rates meant that investors in developing countries were starved for yield. At the same time, Asian countries were growing rapidly, and investors made a beeline to these countries. Foreign capital inflows to Asian countries rose from $9 billion in 1990 to about $80 billion by 1996. This was pure arbitrage to exploit interest rate differentials.—borrow in a low interest rate country and invest in a high rate country. What compounded the problem was a good old maturity mismatch. Asian banks were borrowing short term in dollars and lending long term in their local currencies. 

Suddenly in 1997, the flows reversed, and there were large scale outflows due to a confluence of factors. Companies that had borrowed heavily were unable to roll over their dollar debts. Money channelled into unproductive investments soured, and there was a crisis of confidence which amplified the panic, causing more outflows. All these Asian counties had fixed currencies and were unable to defend as they came under heavy attacks by speculators, given that their foreign exchange reserves were insufficient. Ultimately, these countries were forced to [devalue][7] their currencies, plunging the economies into deep recessions. 

Since these crises, a lot of emerging market and developing economies (EMDEs), particularly Asian countries, come a long way. Many of them are in far stronger positions than they were during the 90s. At the same time, many like the Latin American and Middle Eastern countries remain vulnerable. The problem when talking about EMDE countries is the assumption that they are homogenous. They aren't, there are significant differences in the economic structure of these countries. For example, excluding China when looking at EMs presents a vastly different picture. 

Emerging and developing economies have come a long way since the Latin American and Asian Financial crises. A lot of these countries developed local bonds and developed their investor base to reduce the dependence on the dollar or foreign currency borrowing. Most of the countries abandoned fixed currencies and allowed their currencies to float, albeit with some intervention from their central banks. These also built substantial foreign exchange (FX) reserves to deal with sudden shocks. But progress has been uneven, and several emerging, low income and frontier economies remain vulnerable. 

So, why are people crying wolf about emerging markets? 

Before we get to that, here's a bird's eye view of global and EMEA sovereigns by credit rating trends and outlook. You can dunk on credit rating agencies all you want, but they're still one of the best ways to get a sense of the credit quality of countries and companies. CDS spreads, and bond yields may be better, but data is hard to come by easily 😭

So, the reason why are crying wolf over a debt crisis in emerging markets is because of the COVID-19 pandemic. The entire global economy virtually ground to a halt, and over 90% of all countries suffered a recession. Countries around the world, especially the developed economies, unleashed fiscal stimulus on a scale we have never seen before. Emerging markets economies, too, had to spend [to the extent that they could][11] to support the economy. Given the trillions in fiscal spending across the world, 2020 saw the biggest single year [increase in debt][12] since World War II.

This is every perma bear's dream scenario. They've been screaming that given the increased debt and rising interest payment burdens, the party will end soon and developed and developing economies will become fifth world countries. But a high debt-to-GDP ratio on its own [is quite meaningless][14]. It's a bit like saying the stock market is high. No two countries are the same, and comparing debt to GDP ratios is a fraught exercise. The composition of the debt, the economic growth rates, structure of the economy, institutions, the rule of law, central bank independence, interest rates, and inflation matter more. Just ask all the exotic creatures who've been predicting doom for US and Japan because the debt-to-GDP has been rising since the 2000s.

Anyway, given the record low interest rates around the world since 2008, debt wasn't a problem. With investors hunting for yield, emerging markets saw [inflows][15], barring interspersed episodes such as the taper tantrum in 2013, the China shock in 2015 and the COVID-19 crash in 2020. But now that inflation is back in the US, the Federal Reserve is expected to hike rates aggressively. More worryingly, for the emerging markets, the dollar has been strengthening, and this is a negative.

A [strong dollar][16] is bad for emerging markets, and it hurts the vulnerable EM countries the most.

<img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="650" height="695" src="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Dollar-vs-EM-flows.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-275" srcset="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Dollar-vs-EM-flows.jpg 650w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Dollar-vs-EM-flows-281x300.jpg 281w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 650px) 100vw, 650px" /> IMF 

Boris Hofmann and Taejin Park had published [an analysis in the BIS][17] analyzing the impact of a stronger dollar. They showed that the effect of a rising dollar is more pronounced in emerging markets with higher dollar debt and foreign ownership of local currency bonds. 

<img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="965" height="749" src="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/dollar-vs-EM-growth.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-284" srcset="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/dollar-vs-EM-growth.jpg 965w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/dollar-vs-EM-growth-300x233.jpg 300w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/dollar-vs-EM-growth-768x596.jpg 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 965px) 100vw, 965px" />  

Since the rates in the US stayed low for a long time, the emerging markets sovereign debt doomsayers had to find a second job. But now, the US is finally in a rate hike cycle, these people have rediscovered their purpose in life. But there's some nuance here. 

Jasper Hoek, Emre Yoldas, and Steve Kamin [published a paper][18] looking at the impact of US rate hikes and the impact on emerging markets. They found that if the US hikes rates when economic growth is good, it isn't a problem. But if inflation is rising in the US and the Fed is forced to become hawkish and hike rates, that's bad news for emerging markets. 

A strong dollar is [negatively correlated with global trade][19] and also leads to lower commodity prices as emerging market currencies depreciate and [reduces demand][20]. 

And this is where the problem is. There has been [significant divergence][21] in post-pandemic economic recovery in emerging and developing economies.

<img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="767" height="590" src="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Emerging-markets-and-developed-economies-COVI-19-recovery.png" alt="" class="wp-image-276" srcset="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Emerging-markets-and-developed-economies-COVI-19-recovery.png 767w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Emerging-markets-and-developed-economies-COVI-19-recovery-300x231.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 767px) 100vw, 767px" />  

Commodity exporting emerging countries like Brazil, Mexico, Chile, Saudi Arabia etc., are seeing windfalls on the back of rising commodity prices across the board. But commodity importing countries like Bangladesh, Vietnam, Thailand, and India have taken a growth hit. Tourism dependent countries like Turkey, Thailand etc., have been [hit the hardest][22], and their economic recovery is significantly off the pre-pandemic trend. 

At this point, it's important to understand that not all EMDEs are the same. Nobody even agrees on the definition 

Several of the Asian EMDEs like India, Thailand, South Korea, and Malaysia are in a significantly better place and have built substantial [FX reserves][23]. But low-income developing countries aren't in such good shape. In the recent [World Economic Outlook][24], the IMF noted that _“60 percent of low-income developing countries are already in debt distress or at high risk of distress.”_

Just about 26% of the emerging market debt was denominated in foreign currency, mostly US dollars. Countries like Argentina, Turkey, Mexico, and Columbia were the notable exceptions. Though, emerging economies largely shifted to borrowing in local currencies, some countries had to rely on foreign investors to buy the local debt. About 25% of the local currency sovereign debt is held by foreign investors, mostly open-ended bond funds. 

But this didn't eliminate the _original sin_, it just shifted the rise from borrowers to lenders. Foreign investors have to worry about the appreciation and depreciation of emerging market currencies. If the currency appreciates, it increases the value of emerging market debt investments and flows increase. But if the currency depreciates, it lowers the value of their investments and causes these investors to sell, leading to widening bond spreads. This selling causes a self-reinforcing loop of further selling due to portfolio limits, risk limits, and further widening of spreads <sup><a href="https://www.bis.org/publ/othp32.pdf">1</a>,<a href="https://www.bis.org/speeches/sp211111.pdf">2</a>,<a href="https://www.bis.org/publ/bppdf/bispap113_h.pdf">3</a></sup>.

#### Sovereign piggybanks  

One interesting risk that was highlighted in the IMF's recent Global Financial Stability Report was the substantial increase in the holding of sovereign bonds by emerging market banks post-pandemic. 

<img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="968" height="710" src="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Sovereign-bank-nexus.png" alt="" class="wp-image-306" srcset="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Sovereign-bank-nexus.png 968w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Sovereign-bank-nexus-300x220.png 300w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Sovereign-bank-nexus-768x563.png 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 968px) 100vw, 968px" />  

Banks have been the biggest financed substantial portion of the pandemic driven deficit spending by emerging market countries. Bank holding of sovereign debt is higher in countries with higher debt-to-GDP ratios. The concern highlighted by the IMF is that this link between banks and sovereigns could worsen if the economic growth in emerging markets slowed and if they were to encounter adverse shocks. This would increase sovereign spreads, increase borrowing costs for the governments, losses for banks holding the bonds, higher financing conditions for corporates and other macroeconomic spillover effects. 

#### Sovereign payday lender 

The other risk stems from the fact that China is now the [biggest lender][31] to many small countries across Asia and Africa. It has overtaken the IMF, World Bank and the Paris Club as the [largest creditor to nations][32]. The specifics are unknown, but China through various state owned entities is estimated to have lent between $100-200 billion. This difficulty in assessing the true scale of Chinese lending is dude to the [confidentiality clauses][33] in the debt agreements. 

<img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="834" height="513" src="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/China-lending.png" alt="" class="wp-image-307" srcset="https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/China-lending.png 834w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/China-lending-300x185.png 300w, https://www.bebhuvan.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/China-lending-768x472.png 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 834px) 100vw, 834px" />  

Given the scale of lending, It has been accused of _debt diplomacy_—a honey trap for countries by offering cheap and concessional loans. Given this opacity, some studies estimate that the unreported debts might be around [$385 billion][34]. China has come under increasing criticism and has been blamed for the recent default in Sri Lanka. But not everyone agrees with the notion that China is entrapping countries through debt<sup><a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2021/02/china-debt-trap-diplomacy/617953/">1</a>,<a href="https://www.chathamhouse.org/2020/08/debunking-myth-debt-trap-diplomacy">2</a></sup>.

A lot of these loans are unreported, which makes it's very hard to estimate the exact scale of indebtedness of low income and poor countries. [Carmen Reinhart et al.][35] estimate that 60% of Chinese credit is to countries already in distress. They also note that these debts have led to a wave of [unreported debt restructurings][25], of which little is known about. If these estimates are even half true, it could exacerbate debt distress amongst poor countries. Having said that, China hasn't yet started seizing collateral and taking over countries as many predicted. 

The exact fallout from Chinese lending is anybody's guess. The worry is that this changing composition of creditors to nations could complicate debt resolutions in the event of sovereign defaults. The hope is that China wouldn't risk ruing its reputation by being aggressive in recovering what it's owed, but at this point, hope is all it is.

#### Bottom line 

The bottom line is emerging markets have changed a lot since the Asian and Latin American crises. A lot of these countries, especially the Asian economies, have grown significantly since then and have undergone serious reforms. Barring a few small countries, foreign currency borrowing accounts for a very small share of the total emerging market debt. Emerging markets have also built up a significant amount of foreign currency reserves. 

> Instead of a debt crisis, emerging markets may suffer various degrees of “fiscal scarring”

### Emerging market corporate debt crisis 

It's the same worry when it comes to emerging market dollar-denominated corporate borrowing. People worry about the high corporate debt to GDP ratio in emerging markets. But there's nuance required here. According to [BIS data][39], there's about $4.2 trillion of non-financial dollar debt in emerging markets, sure, sounds scary. But emerging market central banks have built up substantial reserves over the same period, to the tune of $6-7 trillion. 

World Bank researchers in a paper noted that even though corporate debt increased from 56 to 96% of GDP in emerging markets between 2008 and 2018, most of it was in local currency bonds. This mitigates the issue of a strengthening dollar to an extent. The US Federal Reserve conducted a stress test on emerging economies involving falling earnings, rising interest rates and currency depreciation. The analysis found that falling earnings and rising interest rates would be the most disruptive: 

> In particular, the authors assume a fall in earnings of 20 percent, a rise of 100 basis points in the interest rate paid by firms, and a 20 percent depreciation of the exchange rate. This study finds that if the three shocks would occur simultaneously, the debt-at-risk in emerging economies in 2016 would rise from 25 to 41 percent. Excluding China, debt-at-risk would increase from 15 to 34 percent. 

Although high, these values would still be below those on the eve of the AFC when debt-at-risk stood at 48 percent. Nevertheless, in several large emerging economies the share of debt-at-risk would get near or, even, surpass 48 percent. This would be the case of Indonesia (62 percent), Mexico (53 percent), China (48 percent), and Brazil (45 percent). In other words, in major emerging economies about half of nonfinancial corporate debt would correspond to firms with weak debt servicing capacity. According to this Federal Reserve study, shocks to earnings and interest rates would be the
most harmful.
  

  
  
— <a href="https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/handle/10986/34480">Growth of Global Corporate Debt : Main Facts and Policy Challenges</a>

Having said that, not all emerging markets are made the same and aggregates mask substantial variation among emerging market corporates. But the consensus here is that corporates in [China, Latin America][45] and other riskier emerging markets are at the highest risk of defaults. A [simulation by S&P][46] of a 300bps interest rate hike and inflation shock on a sample of 24,445 corporates showed that loss-making companies would increase to 7%, double that of last decade. Regionally, Chinese companies would be the most affected.

The other perennial worry was over the rise of [zombie firms][47]—entities that couldn't cover their interest payments from their earnings and profits. The argument was that a decade of low interest rates had created a massive horde of undead companies because they were able to raise cheap debt by issuing high yield debt. But that doesn't seem to be the case, at least in the [United States][48]. Zombie companies are being upgraded at twice the rate as they are being downgraded. Of course, not everybody thinks that [this will last][48].

The bottom line is that it's highly improbable that we are set to witness a wave of corporate bankruptcies in the emerging markets across as people predict. It goes without saying that a lot of the highly indebted and speculative companies will go down if the US hikes rates dramatically as people are expecting.

So far, the Fed has hiked by 75 basis points and several EMs started way before the Fed. If you look at emerging market bond spreads, the high yield spreads have shot up quite a bit, almost to the levels seen during the COVID-19 crash. So the most speculative and leveraged companies are at the most risk of default. The broader spreads have largely remained in line with historical averages.

### Emerging markets investing  

Emerging markets account for 60% of the world GDP. 80%+ of the world population lives in emerging markets. They have a grown at a faster rate compared to developed economies, the growth premium is 2-3%. Developed markets are aging and increasingly resemble an old age nursing home while emerging markets are full of young people resembling a playground. These are sales pitches you hear as a justification for investing in emerging markets. Has it worked? [Not in a long time][50]. US has been the undisputed leader for well over a decade.
